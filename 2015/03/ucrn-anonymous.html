UCRN is Persistent and is not Anonymous

While writing my response to the Population Register consultation, I came across two bizarre assertions. The first is in the consultation itself:

The UCRN is an anonymous number created by the NRS for each individual on behalf of whom information is contained in the NHSCR.

What on earth is this supposed to mean? The UCRN is a unique number used to refer to a citizen contained in the NHSCR database. Its purpose is to be a numeric name an individual. In what sense is that anonymous?

It turns out that what they mean is explained in the next sentence:

The number contains no personal or identifiable information.

Ok, so the number itself isn't made from your birthdate or anything like that. But this doesn't matter because it is identifial information by definition.

There is nonsense of a similar kind in a related document, the Privacy Impact Assessment for the "myaccount" service. This is meant to be a single-sign-on service for interaction with any part of the Scottish Government and uses the UCRN as the primary key to match records from different databases. Supporting this service is the main motivation for the information sharing that the consultation is about.

The UCRN… is not used as a Persistent Identifier. The UCRN will only be used to match at a high level with a service provider's records.

Hopefully it's clear at least that the UCRN is in no way anonymous, so it is an identifier. What does "persistent" mean? Persistent information is information that sticks around. If you turn your computer off and then on again, whatever you have saved to disk is still there. It is persistent.

If this information is used to match against service provider's records, the service provider needs to maintain a table of (internal identifier, UCRN) pairs. It's hard to believe that this table is not saved. The service would be useless if it were not saved. So it is persistent.

As far as I can tell, the authors of the consultation and the privacy assessment are attempting to redefine the well known words "anonymous" and "persistent" to mean their exact opposites.

The same privacy assessment also contains this statement:

4. Measures taken against individuals as a result of collecting information about them might be seen as intrusive.

The only information held in myaccount will be given to the individual for this purpose. The information does not include any information about service use.

I have quite a lot of trouble believing this. The "myaccount" service is meant to be a trusted authentication source. It receives requests to authenticate users from various places. The history of these requests paints a picture of the interaction of a user with various services. Each time they log in, they leave a footprint that says they accessed a particular service.

If this information is not stored that means there is no logging being done – hardly good news for trustworthiness because this would make it impossible to know anything about how the system is or isn't functioning. The only way that this statement can plausibly be true is if it is interpreted to mean that the logs are not held in myaccount but are held elsewhere by the operator of the myaccount service.

Given that the operator of the myaccount service will then have a history of each individual's interaction with online government services, it is natural to consider this as a kind of surveillance. Surveillance means to watch from above and (usually) to record. As the operator of myaccount must do if it purports to be trustworthy. This is a new service, so the surveillance method is new. And yet the privacy assessment dismisses this out of hand,

3. New surveillance methods may be an unjustified intrusion on individuals' privacy.

Not applicable.

Hardly.
